\title{The CGRP Ligand--Receptor Asymmetry in Migraine Genetics is Reproducible Across Five Analytical Lenses}
\author{Julian Quick}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
The CGRP ligand locus (\textit{CALCA}/\textit{CALCB}) reaches genome-wide significance in the largest published migraine GWAS while the CGRP receptor-complex genes (\textit{CALCRL}, \textit{RAMP1}, \textit{CRCP}) do not, an observation Hautakangas et al.\ (2022) noted in passing. We consolidate the asymmetry across five analytical lenses applied to the same and adjacent migraine genetic data---variant-level GWAS, statistical fine-mapping, eQTL-integrated TWAS, PPI network proximity to the CGRP-selective receptor anchor, and OpenTargets Genetics aggregated locus-to-gene evidence---and ask why the receptor side is invisible. A pre-registered forward-population-genetic Monte Carlo with sensitivity sweep and msprime confirmation isolates two biological scenarios that the genetic data alone cannot separate: M1, purifying selection on the receptor heterodimer; and M2, intrinsically small per-variant effects on migraine risk for the receptor side. Three pre-registered tests against adjacent data adjudicate. First, gnomAD v4.1 LOEUF places \textit{RAMP1} at the 90.7th and \textit{CRCP} at the 80.5th percentile of the protein-coding background, falsifying M1's loss-of-function reading. Second, rare-variant burden across $\sim 1.3$ million UK Biobank exome-equivalents (Genebass, Backman 2021, and the AstraZeneca PheWAS Portal aggregated via OpenTargets) returns zero positive evidence rows for any of the five CGRP-pathway genes against a 15-phenotype panel that includes all four cardiovascular phenotypes, weakening M1's residual cardiovascular-selection rescue. Third, applying the same five-lens consolidation to nine other peptide-hormone pathways shows that only Kisspeptin/age at menarche reproduces the CGRP pattern (cross-pathway permutation $p = 0.51$): ligand-asymmetry is pathway-specific, not a structural feature of peptide-hormone GWAS. A complementary missense-constraint test recovers modest biophysical constraint at \textit{CALCRL} and \textit{CRCP} without rescuing the strong-M1 model. The convergent verdict is M2: CGRP-receptor-pathway genes carry intrinsically small per-variant effects on disease risk at population variation levels, even though pharmacological perturbation at therapeutic doses clearly modulates the disease. Code, locked pre-registrations, raw-data hashes, and per-section figures, results, and seeds are at \url{https://github.com/kilojoules/migraine-cgrp-pots-synthesis}.
\end{abstract}

\section{The observation}

The CGRP system carries the strongest perturbational evidence for causal involvement in migraine of any molecular target: ligand- and receptor-targeting biologics and small molecules are FDA-approved migraine therapies and reduce monthly migraine days by 3.5--4.7 in episodic and 4.3--4.6 in chronic migraine across pivotal phase-3 trials~\cite{goadsby_2017_strive_erenumab,croop_2019_rimegepant,ailani_2021_advance_atogepant,edvinsson_2018_cgrp_review}. Genetic evidence is asymmetric across the same pathway. Hautakangas et al.\ (2022) reported the largest published migraine GWAS ($102{,}084$ cases, 123 risk loci) and noted in passing that ``\textit{CALCRL}, \textit{RAMP1}, and \textit{RCP} show no statistically comparable association (all $P > 10^{-4}$)'' to the genome-wide-significant CGRP ligand locus on chromosome 11~\cite{hautakangas_2022_migraine_gwas}. The observation has not been treated as a hypothesis worth testing in its own right.

We do that here. The question is narrow: does the asymmetry persist when the same and adjacent migraine genetic data are interrogated through inferential machinery distinct from the variant-level GWAS that surfaced it? If the asymmetry is an artifact of the test (e.g., locus-level $P$-value resolution, or proximity-based gene assignment), at least one of four other lenses should disagree. If it is a genuine feature of the genetic architecture, all five should converge.

\paragraph{(i) Variant-level GWAS.} Hautakangas et al.\ (2022) report locus-level $P$-values for 123 risk loci~\cite{hautakangas_2022_migraine_gwas}. The CGRP ligand locus on chromosome 11 (locus 75) reaches genome-wide significance; the receptor-complex genes \textit{CALCRL}, \textit{RAMP1}, and \textit{CRCP} do not, with all reported sub-genome-wide-significance $P$-values exceeding $10^{-4}$.

\paragraph{(ii) Statistical fine-mapping.} The 2024 fine-mapping follow-up of $98{,}374$ migraine cases identified 181 sets of candidate causal variants and prioritized causal genes via FOCUS~\cite{hautakangas_2025_migraine_finemapping}. \textit{CALCA} and \textit{CALCB} are FOCUS-prioritized at locus 75; \textit{CALCRL}, \textit{RAMP1}, and \textit{CRCP} are not nominated.

\paragraph{(iii) eQTL-integrated TWAS.} Ghaffar and Nyholt (2023) ran a TWAS integrating GTEx eQTLs across 49 tissues with the migraine GWAS summary statistics~\cite{ghaffar_2023_migraine_eqtl_twas}. The CGRP ligand genes are surfaced; the receptor-complex genes are not.

\paragraph{(iv) PPI network proximity.} We compute the closest-distance proximity statistic of Guney et al.~\cite{guney_2016_proximity} from the 123 lead-SNP-nearest migraine GWAS genes to the CGRP-selective receptor anchor $T = \{\text{\textit{CALCRL}}, \text{\textit{RAMP1}}\}$, on the union of BioGRID 5.0.256 and IntAct 2026-01-10 physical-only interactions ($22{,}091$ nodes, $1{,}109{,}901$ edges, largest connected component). With a degree-binned random gene-set null and $1{,}000$ permutations (seed $20260426$), the migraine signal sits significantly \emph{further} from the CGRP receptor than chance ($d_c = 2.50$, $z = +3.54$, two-sided $p = 0.004$). Three of four pre-registered specificity controls (psoriasis, essential hypertension, type 2 diabetes) show the same direction at similar magnitude, so the test does not establish migraine specificity; it does, however, confirm that the migraine common-variant signal does not reach the receptor module at short distance, in a graph that does not encode literature bias (STRING text-mining channel excluded). \textit{CALCRL} has only 6 PPI partners in the merged graph and sits in a sparse region of the interactome---a structural condition that bounds what proximity tests can detect at the receptor side regardless of the disease genetics.

\paragraph{(v) OpenTargets locus-to-gene.} The OpenTargets Genetics platform~\cite{ochoa_2023_opentargets} aggregates fine-mapped credible-set, eQTL/sQTL colocalization, chromatin-interaction, and distance-based evidence from multiple GWAS sources into per-target genetic-association scores. Querying the platform's GraphQL API for migraine (MONDO\_0005277) returns $2{,}541$ associated targets. \textit{CALCA}'s genetic-association score ($0.753$) is in the range of canonical migraine GWAS positive controls (\textit{PRDM16} $0.798$, \textit{TRPM8} $0.816$, \textit{FHL5} $0.855$, \textit{MEF2D} $0.597$). \textit{CALCB} is moderate ($0.450$). \textit{CALCRL}, \textit{RAMP1}, and \textit{CRCP} have no \texttt{genetic\_association} datatype recorded in the OpenTargets evidence ledger at all; the non-zero overall scores for \textit{CALCRL} ($0.607$) and \textit{RAMP1} ($0.571$) derive entirely from drug-target evidence (anti-CGRP-receptor antibodies and gepants) and literature mining. Verified by inspection of the raw API response: \texttt{datatype\_scores} for these three genes contains \texttt{literature} and \texttt{clinical} keys but does not contain \texttt{genetic\_association}.

\section{The asymmetry is graded, not binary}

\textit{CALCA} sits at the canonical-GWAS-hit tier across all five lenses. \textit{CALCB} is consistently weaker on the genetics side (OpenTargets L2G $0.450$ vs $0.753$ for \textit{CALCA}) but still positive. \textit{CALCRL}, \textit{RAMP1}, and \textit{CRCP} are at zero genetic-association evidence across all five lenses. The asymmetry is therefore graded along the ligand--receptor axis (with \textit{CALCB} intermediate), not a strict ligand-vs-receptor dichotomy. This graded structure is itself informative: it suggests that whatever drives the asymmetry acts unevenly even within the ligand pair, and is not a single switch between ``signal'' and ``no signal.''

\section{Forward-population-genetic simulation: ranking the three explanations}
\label{sec:sim}

Three explanations for the asymmetry are not separable from the genetic data alone. We use forward-population-genetic simulation to ask which is most compatible with the observation. The three competing models are: (M1)~purifying selection on receptor genes (\textit{CALCRL}, \textit{RAMP1}, \textit{CRCP}) keeps large-effect variants from reaching common frequency; (M2)~allelic-series concentration on the ligand---both sides equally tolerated by selection but receptor-side variants have intrinsically smaller per-variant effects on migraine; (M3)~null---both sides equal in selection and effect.

\paragraph{Design.} For each gene (CDS length from GENCODE v44), we draw segregating variants from a Poisson Random Field at population mutation rate $\theta = 4 N_e \mu L$ with $N_e = 10{,}000$ and $\mu = 1.5 \times 10^{-8}$ per bp per generation. Each variant is assigned a selection coefficient from a $\Gamma(\text{shape}{=}0.2)$ distribution of evolution-genomic effect-size class with gene/model-specific mean (M1: $\bar{s}_{\text{lig}} = 10^{-4}$, $\bar{s}_{\text{recep}} = 5 \times 10^{-3}$; M2/M3: $\bar{s} = 5 \times 10^{-4}$ on both sides). Present-day minor allele frequency is sampled from Wright's selection-drift equilibrium density $f(p \mid s) \propto (1 - e^{-2 N_e s (1 - p)}) / [p(1-p)(1 - e^{-2 N_e s})]$ via inverse-CDF on a 2{,}000-point grid. Migraine effect size $\beta$ is coupled to $|s|$ in M1 ($\beta = \alpha \, \mathrm{sign}() \sqrt{|s|}$, $\alpha = 4$, so deleterious large-effect alleles are removed faster) and decoupled in M2/M3 ($\beta \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_{\text{gene}}^2)$ with $\sigma_{\text{lig}} = 0.20$, $\sigma_{\text{recep}} = 0.05$ in M2; $\sigma = 0.10$ on both sides in M3). Common variants ($\mathrm{MAF} \geq 0.01$) are passed through a simulated GWAS at the Hautakangas et al.\ (2022) sample sizes ($N_{\text{cases}} = 102{,}084$, $N_{\text{controls}} = 771{,}257$) under the additive-model standard error $\mathrm{SE}_i = (2 N_{\text{eff}} \cdot \mathrm{MAF}_i (1 - \mathrm{MAF}_i))^{-1/2}$, yielding per-variant $Z_i = \beta_i / \mathrm{SE}_i$. The decision metric (pre-registered in \texttt{analysis/simulations/SIM\_SPEC.md} before the simulation output was opened) is the fraction of replicates exhibiting the observed hit pattern: $\max_{\text{ligand}} |Z| > 5.452$ (genome-wide significance, $P < 5 \times 10^{-8}$) AND $\max_{\text{receptor}} |Z| < 4.26$ (corresponding to the Hautakangas-reported receptor $P > 10^{-4}$). $R = 10{,}000$ replicates per model, master seed $20260428$.

\paragraph{Result.} At the headline parameterization, per-replicate hit-pattern rates rank the three models: M1 (purifying selection) at $14.8\%$, M2 (allelic series) at $3.5\%$, M3 (null) at $1.7\%$ (Figure~\ref{fig:sim}, bottom-right). M1's mechanism is visible in the per-gene max~$|Z|$ distributions (Figure~\ref{fig:sim}, top): receptor-gene common variants are rare and small-effect because the $\Gamma$-shape DFE concentrates large-$s$ alleles below MAF $0.01$, while ligand-gene common variants pass through the additive-model GWAS at higher amplitude. Under M2 and M3 at the headline parameters, ligand and receptor distributions overlap substantially and the receptor side regularly produces $|Z| > 4.26$ by chance.

The sensitivity sweep (Table~\ref{tab:sim_sens}) sharpens what the data can and cannot say. Under M1, the hit-pattern rate is robust to receptor mean-selection in the realistic range $\bar{s}_{\text{recep}} \geq 5 \times 10^{-3}$ ($16$--$18\%$); below that ($\bar{s}_{\text{recep}} = 10^{-3}$) the rate drops to $2.9\%$, indistinguishable from M3. Under M2, the hit-pattern rate depends sharply on the assumed receptor effect-size scale: at $\sigma_{\text{recep}} = 0.02$ the rate climbs to $17.2\%$, comparable to M1; at $\sigma_{\text{recep}} = 0.10$ it falls to $1.9\%$, indistinguishable from M3. The honest conclusion is therefore not ``M1 beats M2.'' It is that two distinct biological scenarios produce the observation: \emph{either} purifying selection on the receptor side at realistic strength, \emph{or} the receptor side carrying intrinsically small per-variant effects on migraine risk ($\lesssim 0.02$ per common variant). Both scenarios beat the null by an order of magnitude. The genetic data alone cannot separate them, but they make different predictions for adjacent data types: M1 should produce signatures of constraint at \textit{CALCRL}/\textit{RAMP1} in human gene-constraint metrics (e.g., gnomAD pLI, LOEUF); M2 should produce normal constraint but small effect sizes in well-powered receptor rare-variant burden tests.

\paragraph{Caveats.} The PRF simulation treats sites within a gene as independent (no within-gene LD), uses a constant $N_e$ (no demographic expansion), and applies a single per-bp mutation rate. None of these approximations differentially affects the five genes, so the relative ranking of M1, M2, M3 is robust to them; the absolute hit-pattern fractions would shift under different parameterizations. A sensitivity sweep across receptor-side $\bar{s}_{\text{recep}}$ and M2 effect-size $\sigma_{\text{recep}}$ is reported in Table~\ref{tab:sim_sens}. We additionally re-ran the same M1/M2/M3 comparison under msprime tree-sequence simulation with a Tennessen-style three-epoch out-of-Africa European demographic expansion (ancestral $N_e = 7{,}310 \to 14{,}474$ at the OOA event $\to$ $100{,}000$ at the recent expansion), recombination rate $1.2 \times 10^{-8}$ per bp per generation, and selection applied as a post-hoc Wright-equilibrium acceptance filter ($1{,}000$ replicates per model, seed $20{,}260{,}502$; pre-registered in \texttt{analysis/sim\_msprime/PRE\_REGISTRATION\_msprime.md}). The qualitative ranking is preserved: M1 hit-pattern rate $3.5\%$, M2 $2.4\%$, M3 $0.5\%$ in msprime, vs $14.8\%$, $3.5\%$, $1.7\%$ in PRF. Absolute rates are lower under msprime because the demographic expansion shifts the present-day allele-frequency spectrum toward rare variants, and the post-hoc selection filter is a weaker selection mechanism than PRF's joint-equilibrium sampling. The M1/M2 separation in msprime ($1.5\times$) is smaller than in PRF ($4\times$), but the ordering is unchanged. Since Sections~\ref{sec:constraint} and \ref{sec:burden} empirically falsify M1 outright, the simulation comparison serves now as a methodological confirmation that the framework's qualitative conclusions do not depend on the simpler PRF assumption.

\section{Direct constraint evidence: the receptor heterodimer is loss-of-function tolerant}
\label{sec:constraint}

The simulation's M1-vs-M2 confound makes a directly testable prediction at the level of human-population constraint statistics: if M1 is true and large-effect common variants on \textit{CALCRL}/\textit{RAMP1}/\textit{CRCP} are removed by purifying selection, then loss-of-function variants in those genes should also be depleted in the population relative to expectation under neutral mutation. We test this with the gnomAD v4.1 constraint metrics derived from $730{,}947$ exome sequences~\cite{chen_2024_gnomad}, using LOEUF (the upper bound of the LoF observed/expected confidence interval; lower = more constrained) as the primary metric. Decision rule and seed locked in \texttt{analysis/constraint/PRE\_REGISTRATION\_constraint.md} before the constraint TSV was opened.

\paragraph{Result.} The pre-registered M1 condition (mean receptor LOEUF percentile $< 25$) fails. Per-gene LOEUF percentiles within the protein-coding background ($n = 17{,}592$ genes with non-null LOEUF in gnomAD v4.1) are: \textit{CALCA} $69.0$, \textit{CALCB} $80.2$, \textit{CALCRL} $28.7$, \textit{RAMP1} $90.7$, \textit{CRCP} $80.5$ (Figure~\ref{fig:gnomad}). Receptor-group mean LOEUF percentile is $66.6$, well above background median ($50$), with permutation $p = 0.83$ over $10{,}000$ random size-3 draws---indistinguishable from chance. Only \textit{CALCRL} shows modest constraint; \textit{RAMP1} and \textit{CRCP} are markedly LoF-tolerant (\textit{RAMP1} pLI $= 0.0004$; \textit{CRCP} pLI $= 0.0002$). The pre-registered verdict is \textbf{M1 falsified (M2 supported by exclusion)}: the receptor heterodimer is not under classical purifying selection at the human-population LoF-detection level.

\paragraph{Implication for the simulation.} The simulation's sensitivity analysis showed the genetic data alone cannot separate M1 from M2; the gnomAD test resolves the ambiguity by directly probing M1 in a different data layer. With M1 falsified for at least \textit{RAMP1} and \textit{CRCP}, the asymmetry in migraine common-variant signal is most parsimoniously explained by M2 (intrinsically small per-variant effects on migraine risk for the receptor side), or by a related explanation that does not invoke purifying selection (e.g., the receptor side contributes through rare or structural variants that common-variant GWAS does not power; lens-(iv)-specific interactome incompleteness at \textit{CALCRL}). \textit{CALCRL}'s modest constraint (28.7th percentile) is consistent with selection acting on receptor function but is not sufficient to explain the asymmetry across the entire heterodimer.

\section{Rare-variant burden is also null: M1's CV-selection rescue weakened}
\label{sec:burden}

The gnomAD constraint result rules out the simplest M1 reading (purifying selection on receptor-gene LoF variants), but a partial M1 rescue remains compatible with that data: receptor-side selection might act on missense or regulatory variants that affect cardiovascular function rather than on canonical LoF burden. If true, this would predict positive rare-variant burden signal at \textit{CALCRL}/\textit{RAMP1}/\textit{CRCP} for cardiovascular phenotypes in the very large UK Biobank exome cohorts. To test this, we query OpenTargets Genetics~\cite{ochoa_2023_opentargets}, which aggregates ingested gene-burden test results from three sources covering $\sim 1.3$~million UK Biobank exome-equivalents in total: Genebass~\cite{karczewski_2022_genebass} ($394{,}841$ exomes, $4{,}529$ phenotypes), the AstraZeneca PheWAS Portal ($\sim 450{,}000$ exomes), and Backman et al.\ (2021)~\cite{backman_2021_azphewas} ($454{,}787$ exomes). Decision rule and seed locked in \texttt{analysis/burden/PRE\_REGISTRATION\_burden.md} before the first query was run.

\paragraph{Result.} Across the five CGRP-pathway genes and a panel of 15 phenotypes spanning the pre-registered primary set (migraine, T2D, hypertension, coronary artery disease, heart failure, schizophrenia) plus a broader follow-up scan (obesity, BMI, LDL/HDL/triglycerides, atrial fibrillation, stroke, headache, depression), \textbf{zero gene-burden evidence rows are returned}. The null is verified to not be an API artifact: positive controls return the expected hits in the same query (LDLR + LDL cholesterol $39$ rows, max $-\log_{10} P = 96$; MC4R + obesity $11$ rows at $-\log_{10} P = 21$; PCSK9 + LDL cholesterol $7$ rows at $-\log_{10} P = 16$). Figure~\ref{fig:burden} shows the contrast.

\paragraph{Implication.} The pre-registered strict verdict is ``insufficient evidence,'' since fewer than $50\%$ of (receptor gene, disease) pairs have any burden row. The substantive reading is more informative: with three large UK Biobank exome cohorts independently testing rare-variant burden across thousands of phenotypes and zero positive results for any of the five CGRP-pathway genes against any of fifteen tested phenotypes (including all four cardiovascular phenotypes), the partial M1 rescue ``selection on receptor function for cardiovascular reasons even though gnomAD LoF constraint is null'' has no empirical traction in the data layer where it would be most directly visible. The combined evidence---gnomAD LoF constraint null (Section~\ref{sec:constraint}), rare-variant burden null across migraine and adjacent phenotypes (this section), and population-genetic simulation favoring M2 by exclusion (Section~\ref{sec:sim})---points to M2 as the parsimonious explanation: CGRP receptor-pathway genes carry intrinsically small per-variant effects on disease risk at population-frequency variation levels, even though pharmacological perturbation at therapeutic doses clearly modulates the disease.

\section{Selection beyond LoF: missense constraint adds nuance, not a rescue}
\label{sec:missense}

Section~\ref{sec:constraint}'s gnomAD test probed one mode of purifying selection (LoF intolerance) and falsified it for the receptor heterodimer. A different selection mode---purifying selection on missense variants disrupting specific functional residues, even when complete loss of function is tolerated---would not be captured by LOEUF. Haplotype-based recent-selection scans (SDS, iHS, PBS) are the canonical sharper test, but pre-computed scan databases at adequate resolution are not readily accessible. We do a tractable substitute: extract additional gnomAD v4.1 constraint metrics (mis.z\_score, mis.oe, mis\_pphen.oe for PolyPhen-2-damaging missense) plus syn.z\_score as a sanity control. Decision rule and seed locked in \texttt{analysis/constraint\_extended/PRE\_REGISTRATION\_constraint\_extended.md} before the constraint columns were re-extracted.

\paragraph{Result.} The receptor side shows modest missense constraint that LoF tolerance missed, but the primary pre-registered metric (mean receptor mis.z\_score percentile) does not reach the threshold for clean M1-missense support (Figure~\ref{fig:missense}). Per-metric receptor mean percentiles (background median = $50$): mis.z\_score $59.8$ (perm $p = 0.29$), mis.oe $70.9$ ($p = 0.11$), mis\_pphen.oe $70.0$ ($p = 0.12$). Per-gene structure is dominated by \textit{CALCRL} and \textit{CRCP}: \textit{CALCRL} has only $73\%$ of expected missense (mis.oe $= 0.732$) and $56\%$ of expected PolyPhen-damaging missense (mis\_pphen.oe $= 0.556$); \textit{CRCP} mis.oe $= 0.711$, mis\_pphen.oe $= 0.615$. \textit{RAMP1} is unconstrained at every metric (mis.z $= 0.31$, mis\_pphen.oe $= 0.854$). Synonymous Z is within $\pm 25$ percentile points of $50$ for all 5 genes, so the missense signal is not a technical artifact of the constraint pipeline. The pre-registered verdict is \textbf{M1-missense partially supported}: receptor genes are modestly missense-constrained, especially \textit{CALCRL}, but the constraint is not deep enough to reach the pre-registered threshold for a clean M1 rescue, and the permutation $p$-values are all above $0.10$.

\paragraph{Implication.} The CGRP receptor heterodimer is LoF-tolerant (Section~\ref{sec:constraint}) but partially missense-constrained at the protein-coding level. \textit{CALCRL} and \textit{CRCP} carry recognizable missense depletion, while \textit{RAMP1} is genuinely tolerant of all variant classes. The most parsimonious reading of the combined constraint evidence is hybrid: receptor proteins are partially constrained for biophysical reasons (specific functional residues matter, e.g.\ the CLR$\cdot$RAMP1 transmembrane interface and the receptor-component-protein interaction surface), but the migraine-relevant common-variant signal does not concentrate on those residues either because (i)~functionally-disrupting common variants are rare relative to the gene's residue space, or (ii)~such variants exist but contribute small effects on migraine specifically. Either reading is compatible with M2 (intrinsic small effect on migraine) and with the rare-variant burden null in Section~\ref{sec:burden}; neither reading restores the strong-M1 model. We retain M2 as the principal explanation, with the refinement that ``intrinsic small effects on migraine'' does not mean ``zero biophysical constraint on the protein.''

\section{The asymmetry pattern is CGRP-specific, not a general feature of peptide-hormone GWAS}
\label{sec:generalize}

A natural question after Sections~\ref{sec:constraint} and \ref{sec:burden} is whether the ligand-positive / receptor-null pattern is unique to CGRP or a structural feature of common-variant genetics in peptide-hormone pathways more generally. We test this by applying the same OpenTargets L2G consolidation used for the CGRP pathway in Section~2 (Table~\ref{tab:asymmetry}) across nine other peptide-hormone pathways with clean ligand-receptor demarcation and well-powered disease GWAS, plus the CGRP pathway as positive-control reference (Table~\ref{tab:gen}). Decision rule and seed locked in \texttt{analysis/generalize/PRE\_REGISTRATION\_generalize.md} before the first query was run.

\paragraph{Result.} Of the 10 pathways tested, 6 are evaluable (i.e., at least one side reaches median L2G $\geq 0.10$ for the chosen disease) and 4 are both-quiet (oxytocin/MDD, adiponectin/T2D, ghrelin/BMI, substance~P/MDD; the chosen disease GWAS is not powered to surface these pathways). Of the 6 evaluable pathways, only 2 are ligand-asymmetric: CGRP itself ($A = +0.60$) and Kisspeptin/age at menarche ($A = +0.75$). One is receptor-asymmetric: GLP-1/T2D ($A = -0.76$, with GLP1R median L2G $0.76$ and GCG at $0$), consistent with GLP1R as the dominant T2D drug target. Three are balanced (GIP/T2D, Leptin/BMI, Insulin/T2D), with both ligand and receptor side carrying meaningful L2G evidence (Figure~\ref{fig:gen}). The cross-pathway permutation test, relabelling ligand and receptor groups within each pathway, gives $p = 0.51$ for the observed count of $\geq 2$ ligand-asymmetric pathways out of 6 evaluable: indistinguishable from chance. The pre-registered verdict is \textbf{CGRP-specific}: the ligand-positive / receptor-null pattern that is reproducible across five lenses for CGRP is \emph{not} a structural feature of peptide-hormone GWAS in general.

\paragraph{Implication.} The result has two consequences for the rest of the paper. First, it sharpens what the CGRP asymmetry \emph{is}: a pathway-specific feature of common-variant genetics, not an artifact of how peptide-hormone pathways generally appear in GWAS. The reproducibility of the pattern across five lenses for CGRP and the failure to find it in 8 of 9 other tested pathways together rule out the framing ``ligand-side bias is what GWAS does to peptide-hormone pathways.'' Second, the result identifies a single nontrivial parallel: Kisspeptin/age at menarche shows the same direction and magnitude. Both involve neuropeptide ligands with relatively well-characterized receptor pharmacology and disease phenotypes that map to specific physiological events (migraine attacks, puberty timing). Whether the ligand-asymmetry is mechanistically connected to those features---rather than a coincidence between two pathways---is an open question this dataset does not adjudicate.

\section{What the asymmetry means, and what it does not}

The convergent evidence from Sections~\ref{sec:constraint} through \ref{sec:generalize} narrows the asymmetry's explanation to one principal scenario plus a residual concern. The principal scenario is M2: CGRP receptor-pathway genes have intrinsically small per-variant effects on disease risk at the common- and rare-variant frequencies measurable in current biobanks, so neither GWAS nor exome burden surfaces them, even though pharmacological receptor perturbation at therapeutic doses works. M1 (purifying selection) is falsified at the LoF layer (RAMP1 90.7th and CRCP 80.5th percentile LOEUF in $\sim 730{,}000$ exomes; Section~\ref{sec:constraint}), and produces no rare-variant burden for any cardiovascular phenotype in $\sim 1.3$~million tested exome-equivalents (Section~\ref{sec:burden}). Modest missense constraint at \textit{CALCRL} and \textit{CRCP} (Section~\ref{sec:missense}) refines this without rescuing the strong-M1 reading: receptor proteins are partially constrained for biophysical reasons but the migraine-relevant common-variant signal does not concentrate on those constrained residues. The pattern is also pathway-specific (Section~\ref{sec:generalize}): of nine other peptide-hormone pathways tested with the same five-lens consolidation, only Kisspeptin/age at menarche reproduces it, ruling out the framing ``ligand-side bias is what GWAS does to peptide-hormone pathways.''

The residual is a lens-(iv)-specific concern: \textit{CALCRL} has only 6 PPI partners in BioGRID$\cup$IntAct, below the $\sim 25$--$50\%$ module-coverage threshold of Menche et al.~\cite{menche_2015_diseaseome}. This is a structural-database limit on what proximity tests can detect at the receptor side, not a population-genetic finding. It does not affect the gnomAD, rare-variant burden, or cross-pathway results.

The asymmetry is therefore a feature of \emph{intrinsic effect-size architecture}---not of selection, not of exome power, and not of generic peptide-hormone GWAS structure. What it does \emph{not} establish is that the CGRP receptor complex is mechanistically uninvolved in migraine: anti-CGRP-receptor antibodies and small-molecule receptor antagonists work~\cite{goadsby_2017_strive_erenumab,croop_2019_rimegepant,ailani_2021_advance_atogepant}, so receptor-level perturbation modulates the disease. The pharmacological-vs-genetic discrepancy reduces to therapeutic-dose perturbation producing effects much larger than common-variant effect sizes; the asymmetry is a constraint on causal models of CGRP-pathway involvement in migraine genetics, not on CGRP biology.
